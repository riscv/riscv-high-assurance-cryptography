== Executive Summary

Cloud computing has evolved into the foundation of modern digital infrastructure, supporting everything from financial systems to artificial intelligence workloads. At the core of this infrastructure lies cryptography, which protects data, secures communication, and enables trust in distributed environments. Yet, despite its central role, cryptography remains constrained by a persistent and increasingly problematic trade-off: systems can either protect sensitive material effectively or process it efficiently, but rarely both at once.

This trade-off is no longer acceptable.

We propose an architectural solution, called ACE, the _Atomic Cryptographic Extension_, to be integrated into modern Instruction Set Architectures.
ACE is based on the notion of Cryptographic Contexts, protected data structures that encapsulate cryptographic secret, policies for their use, and other state information. These Cryptographic Contexts are used in place of the actual values of the keys by the instructions that perform the cryptographic operations.
Furhtermore, these operations are performed _atomically_, i.e., cryptographic modes and protocols are split into calls to cryptographic primitives but the latter are not themselves split into individual rounds.
This allows systems to perform cryptographic operations at high speed while ensuring their correctness and integrity.

We further argue that, to fully realize this vision in cloud-scale environments, the industry must converge on common standards for representing and managing protected cryptographic material.

== Why This Matters Now

In earlier generations of computing, cryptography was often confined to well-defined boundaries, usually for instance the establishment of secure channels of communication and secure storage for selected applications.
The development of the internet, of mobile client devices, and of cloud computing has significantly expanded these use cases.
Cryptography is now a fundamental component in most data flows, protecting all storage (where files are often encrypted even with device-level encryption enabled), memory (both physical and virtual), most network traffic, and increasingly application logic itself.

This shift is particularly visible in emerging workloads.
Artificial intelligence models are encrypted at rest and often decrypted only at execution time.
Distributed systems rely on encryption and authentication across services.
Confidential computing aims to protect sensitive data even during processing.
In all these cases, cryptography is no longer an occasional operation—it has become part of the steady-state execution of systems.

Consequently, its performance is no longer a secondary concern.
Any inefficiency in cryptographic processing directly impacts latency, throughput, and ultimately the cost and scalability of cloud services.
At the same time, any security weakness in cryptographic processing directly threatens the assets of both cloud service providers and their users.
These two contrasting needs represent a significant tension.

== The Limits of Existing Approaches

=== Specialized Environments

In the early days of computing, the computational complexity of cryptographic operations and the limits of general purpose CPUs have prompted the design of separate hardware cryptographic accelerators.
These have quickly evolved into hardened modules, especially for embedded and high-assurance environments.
The use of such components guarantees a high level of protection of cryptographic secrets.

However, this often comes at a high performance cost: as the speed of general purpose CPUs increased, the overhead of calling such hardware components has become significant, sometimes offseting the performance benefits of using specialized hardware, in particular for smaller payloads.

Therefore, especially when low-latency and responsiveness are required
cryptographic operations have been increasingly often implemented in the firmware or software that requires them.
Such software implementations however expose keys and intermediate values in memory.
This enables serious attacks, for instance through memory disclosure vulnerabilities, or cold boot attacks.

To protect cryptographic keys and sensitive data in this case, existing systems may add an isolation layer, in the form of trusted execution environments or secure enclaves. These are often augmented with protection of the physical memory by means of additional cryptographic layers, such as memory encryption and authentication, but the execution remains confined to the general purpose CPU.
While these mechanisms are effective in reducing the exposure of secrets to general-purpose software, they also come at a performance cost, caused by
the switches to protected software environments and the overhead of the additional protection countemeasures—which sometimes even include disabling the processor caches.  Other factors include the disruption of execution flow, increased cache pressure (if enabled) and increased memory access latency.
In large-scale cloud systems, where millions of cryptographic operations may occur every second, these costs accumulate rapidly.

=== General Purpose Computation Environments

Implementing cryptography in the general execution environment, possibly aided by acceleration instructions such as Intel's AESNI, addresses the performance problem, but, as we have written above, exposes the key to memory or requires additional countermeasure. Therefore, methods for protecting the keys that do not require switches to specialized software of hardware environments each time a cryptographic operation is performed are needed.

Intel's KeyLocker and IBM's Protected Key functionalities go in the right direction by allowing the use of encrypted keys instead of their actual values.
The hardware decrypts them using an internal key when they are used: the values may be set by software, for instance a TEE, or hardware, but are never revealed by the hardware itself.

This leaves a more subtle but equally serious problem unaddressed:
_Only hiding the keys does not necessarily ensure that the desired cryptographic guarantees cannot be broken_.

Complex modes of operation require the confidentiality and integrity also of derived keys and other internal state (e.g., chaining values, counters, masks, intermediate authentication values).
Knowledge of said values may facilitate breaking these modes, or even lead to complete compromises, allowing an attacker to either violate data confidentiality or forge their own messages, regardless of whether the attacker can recover the key.
This applies to modes such as GCM-SIV, OCB, and also the upcoming _accordions_ that will be standardized by the NIST: the security proofs of all such modes rely on the secrecy of all derived keys and values.

Furthermore, current cryptographic accelerators may be configured to work not only with a secret key, but also a set initialization vector, to reduce misuse.
Current _Key-Hiding-Only_ ISAs do not provide such an option.

In effect, protecting only the initial key while leaving the rest of the computation visible is comparable to locking the front door while leaving the shutters of the windows up. While the latter may be made of highly resistant glass, it creates a false sense of security without addressing the full scope of the problem.

Finally, it is customary to define new instructions for each new cryptographic algorithm.
It is clear that this will lead to a proliferation of instructions over time, as various state actors define new standards.
In modern RISC architectures with a single or few instruction lengths, encoding space comes at a premium and defining new instructions requires a thorough review.

_Therefore, we need a solution that limits encoding space usage–ideally, a constant number of instructions, regardless of how many algorithms are supported._

== A Comprehensive Solution

What is needed is not just a more secure way to store and use keys, but a more secure way to think about cryptographic execution as a whole.
Our concept of _Cryptographic Context_ provides such an abstraction.

A Cryptographic Context extends the concept of an encrypted key by including all the information required for a cryptographic operation to be realized as a sequence of smaller operations: the key material, the algorithm that can be used with the key material, any additional values used by the algorithm, the internal state of the latter, and _Metadata_ governing how it may be used. Besides the allowed algorithm, the Metadata also includes usage policies which optionally define usage constraints based on the hardware and software environment, security state, boot cycle, or other policies.
Instead of treating these elements as separate pieces of information that move through the system, the context binds them together into a single protected entity.
Cryptographic Contexts allow us to treat cryptographic algorithms as stateful, encapsulated objects, to which information is passed in a structured manner rather than sequences of stateless primitive invocations.

While software can _provision_ key(s) and Metadata into Cryptographic Context, the latter, once provisioned, cannot be inspected, copied, or modified arbitrarily by software. Instead, it can only be used through well-defined operations that strictly follows the policies allowed by the architecture and by the algorithm.
//In this sense, a cryptographic context behaves like a secure object whose internal structure is opaque but whose functionality is precisely controlled.

Cryptographic Contexts reside in dedicated architectural storage, which we call _Context Registers_.
These are not addressable as normal memory and not directly readable by software.

All ACE operations reference Context Registers rather than the key values.
This limits the visibility of key material to the provisioning phase only, which can be performed by trusted entities.
All computation remains inside a protected hardware domain, and only the final results are visible to software.

It is important that these operations are executed atomically, such as full AES block encryptions and decryptions, steps in modes of operation, and hash absorption rounds.
This prevents leakage from revealing the outputs of intermediate steps: even knowing the input and output to 5 rounds of an AES encryption will significantly facilitate key recovery, therefore we must always compute the full operation.

A Cryptographic Context can be exported from a Context Register to memory as a Sealed Cryptographic Context (SCC), and the latter can be reimported into a Context Register.  A SCC is an encrypted and authenticated representation of the contents of the Context Register, and it preserves all the policies defined in the metadata.
This mechanism uses a _Sealing Key_ which can be securely reconfigured.
It is used to enable secure context switching, and the reconfigurability of the Sealing Key
allows for cryptographic separation of CCs between process domains, and also enables to migrate keys between devices along with their VMs, by migrating the Sealing Key alongside them.

== Advantages

By design, Cryptographic Contexts eliminate many of the traditional sources of vulnerability. Keys and derived values never appear in software-visible form, reducing the risk of leakage through memory inspection or side-channel attacks. The integrity of the context is preserved, ensuring that both the data and the policies governing its use remain intact. At the same time, the architecture can enforce constraints on how a context is used, preventing certain classes of misuse.

Equally important, this model enables cryptographic operations to be executed at high speed. Because there is no need to move data into and out of isolated environments, latency is reduced and throughput increases. The system can take advantage of existing computational resources, including parallelism and vectorization, without compromising security.

Since the choice of the algorithm is embedded in the Metadata, the architecture can offer also offer masked implementations of the same primitives and algorithms to protect against side-channel attacks. The software that uses Cryptographic Contexts does not need to be modified to support side-channel protected operations.

For cloud providers, this combination is particularly valuable. It allows cryptography to be integrated into performance-critical paths—such as storage, networking, and AI inference—without introducing bottlenecks or excessive complexity.

== Call for Action

While the concept of cryptographic contexts provides a powerful foundation, its full potential can only be realized if it is adopted consistently across the industry.  This does not stop at various ISAs adopting it independently:
Today’s cloud environments are heterogeneous, combining multiple processor architectures and hardware platforms, and some providers of keys may be based on a different ISA than the consumers.
Without a common specification, protected cryptographic material becomes difficult to move across these boundaries, limiting flexibility and increasing operational complexity.

First, we need ISAs to expose similar functionality for the use of Cryptographic Contexts, which consumes the same data formats for the initialization of Context Registers and for the SCCs.

Second, there must be a shared understanding of how cryptographic contexts behave. This includes the semantics of the algorithms they implement, the way their state evolves, and the constraints governing their use. A consistent definition ensures that a Cryptographic Contexts created in one environment can be used correctly and securely in another.

== Conclusion

The demand for secure, high-performance cryptography is rapidly increasing, driven by modern computing workloads. Existing approaches force a trade-off between security and performance that is no longer acceptable.

Architectural support for cryptographic contexts offers a path forward: enabling software to perform high-throughput cryptographic operations while ensuring that keys and all sensitive derived values remain protected.

However, realizing this vision at scale requires an industry-wide effort towards standardization—in formats, semantics, and interfaces.
Only through a coordinated effort can we achieve secure, interoperable, and high-performance cryptographic execution across the heterogeneous computing landscape,
without forcing a compromise between efficiency and security.
