[[ACE-unpriv]]
== ACE Unprivileged Architecture

=== Introduction

[[ACE-concepts]]
==== Fundamental Concepts and Definitions

ACE relies on certain fundamental concepts, which are introduced here. While
this leads to some repetitions, this also brings better clarity to the
presentation.

Cryptographic Context:::
(((Cryptographic Context)))
The *Cryptographic Context* (CC) is the foundation of ACE. A CC is an indivisible structure consisting of a *Metadata* section and a *Content* section; all cryptographic operations performed by ACE execute within the constraints defined by a CC. The Metadata has a fixed format and specifies the cryptographic algorithm and various optional policies associated with the CC.
(((Cryptographic Context, Metadata)))
The Content has an algorithm-specific format and holds sensitive material such as keys and internal algorithm state.
(((Cryptographic Context, Content)))
The Metadata requires integrity; the Content requires both integrity and confidentiality. These requirements are architecturally enforced by ACE and by the cryptographic algorithms it implements.

Context Register:::
(((Context Register)))
To create CCs, ACE uses *Context Registers* (CRs), per-hart architectural containers that safeguard their contents. A CR is *provisioned* by writing Metadata and Content into it; this operation binds the latter to form a CC.
There is no store-like instruction to extract the Content of the CR in a manner that compromises its confidentiality. A CC may be used for cryptographic operations only while resident in a CR. Only the ACE unit may access or modify a CC residing in a CR, in accordance with architectural rules and the algorithm bound to the CC.

Sealed Cryptographic Context:::
(((Cryptographic Context, sealed)))
To support context switching while at the same time protecting CCs outside architectural state, a CC may be *exported* from a CR as a *Sealed Cryptographic Context* (SCC), an encrypted and authenticated representation of a CC that can later be *imported* into a CR. SCC export and import use a _Context Sealing Key_ (CSK), which may be programmable by M-mode.
A CC sealed under one CSK cannot be re-imported under a different CSK, enabling cryptographic domain separation across spatial and temporal domains.

Object-Orientation:::
(((Cryptographic Context, object-oriented)))
CCs act as *objects* exposing a uniform interface across algorithms.
A CC encapsulates algorithm-specific behavior, enabling interchangeable use of different primitives (e.g., AES-128, AES-256, SM4) in identical modes (e.g., ECB, GCM-SIV, XTS) without requiring changes to compiled code.
Side-channel attack (SCA) resistant variants are supported transparently.

Usage Control Policy:::
(((Cryptographic Context, usage control)))
(((Cryptographic Context, management operations)))
(((Cryptographic Context, usage operations)))
ACE distinguishes between CC/CR *management* and *usage* operations.
(((Cryptographic Context, export)))
Management operations include *configuration* (e.g., CR provisioning, SCC import, and cloning) and *export* of a CR as an SCC. Usage operations include cryptographic operations and state changes.
(((Cryptographic Context, access)))
All operations represent a form of *access* to CRs.
To prevent denial-of-service scenarios, management operations, in particular configuration operations, are far less restricted than usage operations. _Usage Control_ policies restrict only certain CC/CR usage operations.

Localities:::
(((Cryptographic Context, Locality)))
ACE can bind CCs to a *Localities*, i.e., restrict usage to specific devices or device classes, hardware configurations, physical or virtual boot cycles, and process domains at various privilege modes. This binding is achieved by using the Localities as _tweaks_ in the encryption and decryption of SCCs.

[[ACE-architectural-model]]
==== Architectural Model

ACE is architected as an _attached_ unit.
This allows implementations both within each core and as a block shared by multiple cores, with a single instance of the hardware and one separate architectural state for each hart.
The present specification mandates only its behavior, not its implementation.

Consequently, ACE instructions behave as messages over a tightly coupled coprocessor interface.
The ACE unit accepts operations, executes them independently of the issuing hart, and later returns results.
To enforce this decoupling, the ACE unit may lock certain architectural states, for instance Context Registers and CSRs, causing the hart to wait when attempting to read them.

This design permits a certain degree of concurrency: ACE operations may proceed even while the control flow of the hart is preempted, and long-running cryptographic computations are allowed. Certain interrupts, however, may be permitted to suspend ACE operations.

For example, let us consider a U‑mode process executing an ACE instruction that modifies only internal state, say, a vector register.
If the process is preempted, the ACE unit may continue its work until the context‑switch handler attempts to access that register, at which point the hart would wait for the operation to finish.
Alternatively, an implementation may adopt a more precise exception model: halting the ACE unit, setting `acestart` to allow later resumption. Optionally the implementation may impose a timeout before halting precisely.
The architecture permits all these approaches and fully specifies the behavior of ACE instructions when interrupted at admissible points.

Furthermore, the following model will help understanding the architecture while reading the spec.
From the logical point of view, for each of the 32 CRs, ACE maintains an entire 64-bit Metadata Section
as well as an offset into an internal memory for the storage of the Content of each configured CR.
If a CR is not configured, the information in the Metadata Section will reflect that.
The actual implementation may considerably differ.

[[ACE-dependencies-unpriv]]
=== Dependencies on Other Extensions

ACE inputs and outputs may be conveyed through vector registers or through an architectural input/output buffer (ACEIOBUF) loadable from and storable to memory; these two mechanisms are not mutually exclusive.

When vector registers are used, the full V extension is not required. ACE depends only on a minimal subset, [.red]#which we shall define in due time#, denoted RVV-mini.
(((RVV-mini)))
RVV-mini must be fully opcode-compatible with RVV: code written for RVV-mini must execute with identical behavior under RVV. The required features are:

* Vector registers wide enough to support the inputs and outputs of the
  implemented algorithms;
* The `vsetvli` instruction with restricted parameters (e.g., `vsetvli
  zero,4,e32,m1,ta,ma` for 128-bit operations);
  unsupported configurations must raise an illegal instruction exception;
* Unit-strided vector loads and stores; and
* Vector bitwise logical operations: `vxor`, `vand`, and `vor`.

ACE instructions (currently) do not use vector masking and do not define masked versions of `ace.exec`.
Therefore, the algorithms currently defined require unmasked inputs and outputs.

The ACE unit is agnostic to `VL` and `SEW`, but not to their product `VL*SEW` (or the length `aceiobuftop` of the ACEIOBUF in bits).
This means that while the CPU instruction-decoding side of ACE will use `VL` and `SEW` to determine the amount of data to send to the “ACE Unit”
side of the implementation, the latter will need only to know the product `VL*SEW` (or `aceiobuftop`) for its functionality.

Floating-point, fixed-point, and general arithmetic vector operations are not required.
Complex operations for certain cryptographic algorithms may be performed in GPRs, with data inserted into vector registers and result
extracted from them via `vins`/`vext`.

[WARNING]
RVV-mini does not need to be formally defined before ACE ratification begins; it
may be defined later, with the ACE specification amended accordingly.
This allows additional requirements to be collected, for example whether to mandate
`vclmul.v[vx]` and `vclmulh.v[vx]` from `Zvbc`.

=== Programmer-Visible Architectural State

[[ACE-cryptographic-registers]]
==== Context Registers

{empty}
(((Cryptographic Context)))
ACE defines up to 32 Context Registers (CRs), numbered 0 to 31 and denoted `K0`–`K31`. CRs are architectural, i.e., per-hart.
(((Context Register File)))
CRs reside in a dedicated, per-hart _CR file_ (CRF) accessible only to the ACE implementation.

In ACE instructions, a CR may be addressed directly, as a 5-bit immediate, or indirectly, via a GPR value.
Accessing a CR with an index outside [0..31] raises an illegal instruction exception.

Unlike a conventional register, a CR can be _unconfigured_, meaning it holds no information.

We summarize the most important operations on CRs:

* Metadata and Content are loaded into a CR via `ace.prov` (<<ACE-instruction-init>>).
* A CR is cleared and unconfigured using `ace.clear` (<<ACE-instruction-clear>>).
* `ace.export` (<<ACE-instruction-export>>) exports a CR to memory as an SCC.
* An SCC may later re-imported into a CR via `ace.import` (<<ACE-instruction-import>>).  Import may fail if the SCC has been modified.
* `ace.exec` (<<ACE-instruction-exec>>) performs cryptographic operations on data blocks according to the algorithm configured in a CR.
* `ace.setst` (<<ACE-instruction-setst>>) transitions between states of a cryptographic algorithm.

[NOTE]
====
CRF capacity consumed by a CR depends on the configured algorithm; two CRs may
consume different amounts. Insufficient residual CRF capacity may prevent
execution of a configuration instruction (`ace.prov`, `ace.import`, `ace.clone`
(<<ACE-instruction-clone>>); software must then free capacity by unconfiguring
other CRs.

CRF capacity is implementation-defined. Implementations must provide sufficient
capacity for all supported operations. At a minimum, the CRF must hold the
largest possible single CR, or the largest CR pair used in cloning operations.
An optional, implementation-specific mechanism for estimating required capacity
is described in <<ACE-instruction-size>>.
====

[NOTE]
====
Indirect CR addressing has two microarchitectural implications:

. Adding CR renaming would significantly increase microarchitectural
  complexity. However, CRs are redefined far less frequently than GPRs or FP
  registers (internal implied changes aside), so the absence of CR renaming is
  not expected to cause performance issues.
. An additional bit in the instruction encoding selects between a 5-bit
  immediate CR number and a GPR-supplied CR number.
====

[WARNING]
====
VM migration can become an issue when the destination machine has a smaller CRF
than the source machine. Migration could fail, or the VM may have to let some
processes terminate abnormally. A lazy-loading mechanism for CRs, besides
optimizing the performance of context switching, may be used to load CRs on
demand; see <<ACE-lazy-loading>>.
====

[[ACE-iobuf]]
==== ACE Input/Output Buffer

The preferred mechanism for supplying inputs to ACE operations and collecting
their outputs is the vector register file of the V extension. ACE also defines
an optional architectural, per-hart input/output buffer (ACEIOBUF) that may be
used in place of vector registers, as described in <<ACE-usage-input-output>>.
The ACEIOBUF is not part of any CR. It may reside within CRF memory or in its
own memory region internal to the ACE state. Support for vector registers and
for the ACEIOBUF is not mutually exclusive.

The total length and the range of the ACEIOBUF used in cryptographic
instructions are controlled by the `aceiobuflen` (<<ACE-CSR-aceiobuflen>>) and
`aceiobuftop` (<<ACE-CSR-aceiobuftop>>) CSRs.

[[ACE-Algorithm-field]]
===== _Algorithm_ and _AlgorithmPolicy_ Fields

Algorithms are encoded in an 11-bit field. Values 0–1535 are
architecture-defined; the list of encodings is maintained by RVI (see
<<ACE-algorithms>>). The remaining values are available for custom algorithms.

For encryption/decryption primitives and modes, the _AlgorithmPolicy_ field
indicates whether the CC may be used for encryption (lower bit set), decryption
(upper bit set), or both. At least one of the bits must be set.

For asymmetric primitives supporting signatures, the lower bit permits
signature generation and the upper bit permits verification.
Note that such an asymmetric primitive could be configured to support
primitives such as key agreement or key encapsulation but _not_ signatures,
and in this case both bits are allowed to be zero.

For other primitives, the _AlgorithmPolicy_ field may encode additional
algorithm identifiers to expand the effective encoding space of the _Algorithm_
field.

[[ACE-State-field]]
===== _State_ Field

The _State_ field shall be zero in a PI.
It used to track the currently state of an algorithm, including errors.

Two types of errors may occur as a result of executing ACE instructions:

. Errors occurring in the instructions themselves or because wrong instructions are issued.
. Errors arising from the correct execution of the algorithms, as well as any other exceptional situations occurring despite the correct instructions being performed.

In general, the first type of errors generates an exception, while the second type will cause an error code to be reported directly to the user software.
There are however exceptions to this rule, as well as conditions that may be reported either way.

The architected values of _State_ which are common to all algorithms are defined in
<<ACE-states-valid>>.

[[ACE-states-valid]]
.Global CC State Numbers: Valid States
[float="center",align="center",width="100%",cols="^7%,<24,<15%,<54%",options="header"]
|===
| Value | Mnemonic                  | State Name     | Description
|   0   | `ace_state_off`           | _Off_          | The CR is not configured.
|   1   | `ace_state_initial`       | _Initial_      | The state of a CC immediately after provisioning or reset; ready for operation.
|  21   | `ace_state_success`       | _Success_      | Algorithm completed successfully. No further cryptographic operations may be performed until the CC is reset.
|  22   | `ace_state_failure`       | _Failure_      | Failure in an algorithm, usually an authentication failure in AE(AD) algorithms or a signature verification failure. No further cryptographic operations may be performed until the CC is reset.
|===

[[ACE-states-error]]
.Global CC State Numbers: Interrupted and Error States
[float="center",align="center",width="100%",cols="^7%,<24,<15%,<54%"]
|===
|  23   | `ace_state_interrupted`   | _Interrupted_   | Provisioning or import interrupted.
|  24   | `ace_state_expired`       | _Expired_       | The CR has expired.
|  25   | `ace_state_import_auth`   | _Auth_Failed_   | Authentication failure with `ace.import`.
|  26   | `ace_state_priv_viol`     | _Priv_Viol_     | Violation of Usage Control policies.
|  27   | `ace_state_out_of_mem`    | _Out_of_Memory_ | Insufficient CRF memory for configuration instruction. This may be set by privileged code in case they do not handle the CR allocation.
|  28   | `ace_state_not_impl`      | _Not_Impl_      | Not implemented algorithm. It must be set by privileged software if it does not implement the algorithm.
| 29, 30 | (Reserved)               |                 | Reserved for future use.
|  31   | `ace_state_invalid`       | _Invalid_       | Invalid/invalidated state, for instance because of the use of an invalid value (argument, state, or metadata).
|===

The state with value 0 is termed _Off_, and is used to indicate that the CR is not configured.
The Metadata Section in this case is the all-zeros value `zeros(64)`.

All states in the range 1-22 are termed _valid_. This includes both _Success_ and _Failure_ states, which must be used for
normal termination of an algorithm in the cases of its successful completion or failure.
The states in the range 24-31 are termed _error_ states, and are used to report certain specific ACE errors.

State 23, i.e., _Interrupted_, is used to report that provisioning or import was interrupted, and is handled differently from the error stated.

Unless an algorithm's specification explicitly states otherwise, a transition from any valid state to _Initial_ using instruction `ace.setst` is always permitted.
A transition from any state, including _Off_, to _Initial_ is only permitted as the result of provisioning or import.

Transition from an interrupted or error state to _Initial_ is not allowed.

When a CR transitions to an error state, the content of the CR becomes invalid and is erased.
The corresponding internal resources may also be released, so that the CR's only information is the Metadata Section.
In this case, exporting the CR will only export the metadata, and the SCC will be just 24 bytes long.
The resulting SCC can be reimported, so that user software may be able to take the necessary actions even after a context switch.

If an algorithm defines 22 or fewer valid states (including the three fixed valid states _Initial_, _Success_, and _Failure_),
the current state is stored in the _State_ field. For algorithms with
more than 22 valid states, additional state information is stored in the Content
section. In all cases, the _State_ field shall be set to _Initial_ (1),
_Success_ (21), _Failure_ (22) or any of the values in the range 23-31 when the CC is in the corresponding state
These states shall be unambiguously identifiable solely from the _State_ field,
without inspecting the Content.

Attempts to _use_ a CR in an error state, be it invalidated or expired, will
not perform any operation in the ACE unit, and in particular they will leave
the _State_ field (now containing an error code) unchanged.

Any _usage_ instruction that would violate the Usage Control policies will
not perform any operation in the ACE unit and set _State_ to `ace_state_priv_viol`.

If an algorithm is not implemented in the ACE hardware and is instead emulated by software, an exception is raised with `mcause` = `ace_exc_not_impl`.
(cf. <<ACE-exception-codes>>).
If the software stack does not implement the algorithm, it must set the _State_ metadata field to `ace_state_not_impl` and return.

During `ace.prov` and `ace.import`, _State_ is set to _Interrupted_ for the
duration of the operation and updated to the correct value only upon successful
completion (see <<ACE-provisioning-export-import>>). By the rules of
<<ACE-invalidation>>, a partially provisioned or imported CR cannot be
exported. If a context switch occurs during such an operation, privileged code
must unconfigure the CR; upon resumption, the instruction is restarted. An
invalid CR with a nonzero `acestart` is recognized as an incomplete CR,
permitting resumption of provisioning or import. This state change to _Interrupted_
does not occur during `ace.export`.

Explicitly setting an error state can be done using `ace.setst` (cf. <<ACE-instruction-setst>>) without additional parameters.

Error conditions that lead to exceptions are treated in <<ACE-CSR-ace-error>>.

[[ACE-Localities]]
===== _Locality_ Field

ACE maintains a table of 128-bit Locality Secrets (LS), the Locality Secrets
Table (LST). Some entries are fixed or configurable only via an
implementation-specific authenticated hardware procedure; these are global and
shared across all ACE units in the same SoC. Other entries are per-hart
architectural state programmed by privileged modes.

<<ACE-locality-indexes>> lists the architected Localities. Eleven LST entries

[[ACE-extra-content]]
===== _AddDataSize_ Field

The _AddDataSize_ Metadata field records the length of the _Additional Data Section_ of the content, which is a variable-size part which may
depend on the state of the algorithm.

The format of the _Additional Data Section_ is described in <<ACE-data-formats>>.

The first eight bytes of the Implementation-Dependent Additional Data Section of the Content, if not empty, always hold a copy of the `acemarchid` CSR (<<ACE-CSR-acemarchid>>) of the originating machine, and are counted within the length recorded by the _AddDataSize_ field.
The importing implementation uses this value to determine whether any information in the Implementation-Dependent Data Section can be used.

It is the responsibility of the *algorithm* to determine the lengths of the two parts of the Additional Data Section.
If the Implementation-Dependent Additional Data Section is not empty, the *implementation* will check the value of `acemarchid` stored therein to decide whether the Implementation-Dependent part can be used or should be ignored.

[NOTE]
====
Examples of Additional Data are:

. “Decryption AES keys”, i.e., the last one or two AES round keys, from which one can work backwards when performing AES decryptions.
. Precomputed point multiples for elliptic curve scalar multiplication (their computation can be far more expensive than their
encryption and decryption);
. Information, such as additional elliptic curve points or random numbers used by Elliptic Curve algorithms only in certain states;
. any auxiliary data for side-channel-protected implementations, such as key shares or other data to accelerate share reconstruction.

Examples 1., 2, and 4. are optional data, and therefore should be stored in the _Implementation-Dependent_ Additional Data Section.

The third example, however, describes information that is required for the correct functioning of the algorithm. Therefore, its format must be defined by the algorithm specification and the data should be stored in the _Architected_ Additional Data Section.
====

[[ACE-Metadata-expiration-date]]
===== _ExpirationDate_ Field

If non-zero, _ExpirationDate_ specifies the CC expiration time as a number of
hours elapsed since the base date of January 1, 2027. The 18-bit field provides
for a span of approximately 30 years (2^18^ hours). Future 128-bit metadata formats
(encoded with _Algorithm_ = `ones(11)`) may support expanded time spans.

[[ACE-data-formats]]
==== Formats of Provisioning Input, Sealed Cryptographic Context, and Content

The format of the Provisioning Input (PI) is shown in <<ACE-provisioning-input>>.

[[ACE-provisioning-input]]
.Format of the Provisioning Input (PI), sections in order
[options="header"]
|===
| Section                       | Size | Description
| Metadata Section             | 64 bits | Contains the metadata information as described in <<ACE-Metadata>>.
| Provisioning Content         | Depends only on the _Algorithm_, _AlgorithmPolicy_, and _KeyType_ fields of the Metadata Section. | Contains keys and other algorithm-specific information.
|===

The CR itself may contain significantly more information than the Provisioning Content.
It is important to understand that this information may be categorized under different classes.

Some information inside a CR may be easily reconstructed from other information, so there is no need to export it.
In this case, an *algorithm* may choose to not include it into an SCC.

Other data may be useful for performance purposes on some implementation and be expensive to recompute, more expensive than encrypting and storing it externally. In this case, an *implementation* may choose to export it in a custom format inside a SCC, to be able to reuse it efficiently, whereas the same data may be safely ignored by a different implementation if the latter receives the same SCC for import.

[options="header"]
|===
| Section                       | Size | Description
| Metadata Section              | 64 bits | Contains the metadata information as described in <<ACE-Metadata>>.
//
| Content: _Fixed Data Section_ (Fixed Content)

The architected order of the sections of the SCC guarantees that the sizes of the two components can be determined while reading and decrypting an SCC.

[[ACE-CSR-ace-error]]
=== Errors and Exceptions

[WARNING]
Errors which are a functional result of the execution of an ACE instruction
will not trigger a trap. However, there are other ACE instructions
which can trigger trap besides providing a functional error. Traps have higher
priority than functional errors. `ace.export`, `ace.import` and `ace.prov` are
able to trigger traps as they interact with the memory system.  Every other
functional ACE error is written to `state` field of the metadata.

[WARNING]
Migrate part of this to the Privileged Architecture chapter?

When an exception is raised, the CSR `mcause` will reflect the error condition.
<<ACE-exception-codes>> lists `mcause` values defined by ACE.
The error codes are listed in decreasing priority order.
The actual numbers are TBD.

Attempts to _use_ a CR in an error state, be it invalidated or expired, will
not perform any operation in the ACE unit, and in particular they will leave
the _State_ field (now containing an error code) unchanged.
It is the responsibility of the caller to handle the error condition.

A violation of Usage Control policies will set the _State_ field to `ace_state_priv_viol`.

If there is insufficient CRF memory for a configuration instruction, the ACE instruction
raise exception `ace_exc_out_of_mem`.
It is up to the system software to manage the CRs by freeing internal ACE Unit space
and retry the instruction.

Errors are returned by setting an error code in the _State_ field of the Metadata Section of the CR.

Attempting to configure a CR with a not implemented algorithm will
raise exception `ace_exc_not_impl`, and the privileged code may either set
the metadata _State_ field to `ace_state_not_impl`, or implement its operations
in software, by trapping the `ace.exec` and `ace.setst` instructions and emulating
them in software when they are used with that CR.

If an expired CR is imported, provisioned, or used, the _State_ field of the
Metadata is immediately set to `ace_state_expired`, and the instruction raises
exception `ace_exc_expired_CR`. Privileged code may then take appropriate
action, such as provisioning a new key or returning to the caller for suitable
handling.

[[ACE-CSR-acestart]]
==== `acestart`

{empty}
(((CSR, `acestart`)))
`acestart` is an alias for `vstart`. Therefore, it is an XLEN-bit RW CSR. ACE
defines it also when the V extension is not enabled.

[WARNING]
[red]#`acestart` is currently defined as an alias for `vstart`.
The reason for this is that the ISA (in fact, almost all ISAs) expose a model
of computation whose semantics correspond to instructions being executed in-order
and one instruction starting only after the previous one has completed.
The current point of execution is given by the Program Counter (PC). Therefore,
only one instruction may be interrupted for resumption in such an architecture,
and this includes the RISC-V vector instructions. If the implementation is
executing other instructions past the PC, these are discarded, and will have to
be completely resumed upon returning from an interrupt.#

`acestart` tracks the progress of interruptible ACE instructions, namely
`ace.prov`, `ace.import`, `ace.export`, `ace.input`, and `ace.output`. It also
applies to `ace.exec` when iteratively processing a multi-block operand, as
`ace.exec` may be interrupted between blocks. Only these instructions honor a
nonzero `acestart`; all other instructions ignore it and are restarted, WITH
acestart[XLEN-9:0] also being cleared. Provisioning, import, and export are
resumed only under certain conditions, as explained in
<<ACE-provisioning-export-import>>.

The bottom XLEN-8 bits of `acestart` record the progress of an interrupted
operation. Since ACE does not have an _intrinsic_ concept of element length,
`acestart`[XLEN-9:0] counts the number of bytes processed so far, not the
number of elements. The top 8 bits of `acestart` are used to encode additional
information and are ignored if not used.

`acestart`[XLEN-1:XLEN-8] is non-zero only if the interrupted instruction is
`ace.prov`, `ace.import`, or `ace.export`, in which case it is equal to
`acestart`[XLEN-1:XLEN-8] = `3'b100 @ bin(n,5)` where `n` is the number of the
CR being provisioned, imported, or exported at the time of interruption.

Hardware typically writes this register on interruption. Software may write
zero to force a full restart and may save and restore the register across
context switches. Writing an invalid or non-supported nonzero value may raise
an illegal instruction exception.

When a vector instruction is interrupted, the CSR follows `vstart` semantics,
i.e., it counts the number of processed elements. When an ACE instruction is
interrupted, the CSR follows `acestart` semantics described here.

[[ACE-CSR-aceiobuftop]]
==== `aceiobuftop`

{empty}
(((CSR, `aceiobuftop`)))
`aceiobuftop` is an XLEN-bit URW CSR that marks the upper bound of the buffer
range used by `ace.input` and `ace.output`. Together WITH
acestart[XLEN-9:0][XLEN-9:0], it defines the active transfer window.

The constraints `acestart[XLEN-9:0]` {le} `aceiobuftop` {le} `aceiobuflen` must
hold; violating either bound raises an illegal instruction exception.
Modifying `aceiobuftop` does not alter or invalidate buffer contents.

Using ACEIOBUF when `aceiobuftop` − `acestart[XLEN-9:0]` is zero (with
`aceiobuflen` nonzero) or an invalid transfer size raises an
illegal instruction exception

NOTE: `aceiobuftop` enables per-operation length adjustments without
reconfiguring the ACEIOBUF, which can be expensive due to the fact that writing
to `aceiobuflen` clears the buffer and may lead to internal garbage collection.
This is useful to process an incomplete “last block” or to supply a short IV or
nonce.

NOTE: The dirtiness of ACEIOBUF is not tracked. We recommend software to set
`aceiobuftop` to zero once the contents of the ACEIOBUF are no longer needed.
Context switching code can use this information to avoid unnecessary save and
restore of ACEIOBUF contents.

[[ACE-instructions-detailed]]
=== Instructions

[WARNING]
====
The encodings presented here are preliminary.
====

ACE provides a set of unprivileged instructions, some of which are implemented
as CSRRW operations. This section specifies their semantics and encodings.

The notation `K(Xd)` denotes the CR whose index is held in GPR `Xd`. `Kn|K(Xn)`
indicates two alternative encodings: a 5-bit immediate CR index (`Kn`) or a CR
number supplied by GPR `Xn`. Use of the zero register `X0` as a CR index
register raises an illegal instruction exception unless explicitly indicated
otherwise.

Each instruction is characterized by the following properties:

* _Modifies state_: whether the instruction may modify ACE architectural state;
* _Usage-controlled_: whether the instruction is subject to usage control
  policies (_UsagePolicy_);
* _Trappable_: whether the instruction may be implemented by privileged code
  via trap-and-emulate; and
* _Interruptible_: whether execution may be interrupted (e.g., by a context
  switch or page fault) and later resumed or restarted.

NOTE: Trappability refers solely to the CPU-side implementation—for example, to
enable decomposition of certain ACE instructions into smaller custom
operations. This is distinct from custom algorithm implementations within the
ACE unit itself (e.g., via a secure embedded CPU with updatable firmware),
which are outside the scope of this specification.

NOTE: ACE does not define the concepts of
Element Group Width, Effective Element Width,
and
Element Group Size as the vector crypto extension does.
Because of the fact that the architecture decouples instructions like `ace.exec` from the
actual operations, the high level architecture of ACE only needs to know the total size
of the inputs and output, which is `ACELEN`, namely either `VL*SEW` or the length
`aceiobuftop` of the ACEIOBUF as defined in <<ACE-notation>>.

[[ACE-instruction-init]]
==== ace.prov

_Synopsis_::
Provision Metadata and secrets into a CR.

_Mnemonic_::
`ace.prov Kd|K(Xd), %offset(Xs)`
(((ACE, instruction, ace.prov)))

_Encoding_::
`ace.prov` is an I-type instruction.
+
[wavedrom, , svg]
....
{reg:[
    { bits:  7, name: 0x0b, attr: ['custom-0'] },
    { bits:  5, name: 'context', attr: ['Kd','K(Xd)'] },
    { bits:  3, name: 'ace.prov', attr: [0x4,0x5] },
    { bits:  5, name: 'Xs' },
    { bits: 12, name: 'immed12', attr: ['offset'] },
]}
....

_Description_::
`ace.prov` provisions a CR with Metadata and Content to create a CC. `Kd` or
`K(Xd)` denotes the CR to be provisioned. `Xs` points to the region of memory
containing the Metadata and the key material. The PI must be stored at an
8-byte aligned address, and its length must be a multiple of 8 bytes.
+
If the instruction fails, the target CR is unconfigured, with the same
semantics as if `ace.clear` were invoked on the same CR
(<<ACE-instruction-clear>>).
+
Exception `ace_exc_not_impl` is raised if the combination of _Algorithm_,
_AlgorithmPolicy_, and _SCProtection_ is not supported.
The handler may simply set the CR to error state `ace_state_not_impl` and
return to the original control flow.
+
Exception `ace_exc_invalid` is raised if the Metadata is invalid (other
than unimplemented algorithm), such as, for instance: a nonzero _State_ or
reserved field; or an unsupported _Locality_ value.
The handler may simply set the CR to error state `ace_state_invalid` and
return to the original control flow.
+
Similarly to vector store instructions, `ace.prov` appears to other harts as a
sequence of individual reads.
+
The operation is described in detail in <<ACE-provisioning-export-import>>.

_Properties_::
Possibly modifies state: *YES*. +
Usage-controlled: *NO*. +
Trappable: *YES*. +
Interruptible: *YES*. +

_Description_::
+
--
This instruction can be used to:

* Activate the side-channel protection of the CC or increase it;
* Restrict the use of encryption or decryption in a CC that allows both;
* Restrict the usage of a CC (as stored in a CR) to specific modes; and
* Add a Locality or replace a Locality with a stricter one.

Let `mh` be the Metadata Section of the CC in the destination CR.

On RV64, the semantics of the instruction are:

* `Xs` follows the format of the Metadata Section.
Only the fields _AlgorithmPolicy_, _SCProtection_, _Locality_, _UsagePolicy_,
and _ExpirationDate_ of `Xs` may be defined. All other fields must be 0, else
exception `ace_exc_invalid` is raised.

[WARNING]
We implement `ace.clear` by setting a special value through `ace.restrict`, such
as a non-zero value in the _State_ field, which this instruction cannot normallyset.
The result is the clearing of the CR.
If the encoding with indirect CR addressing is used and Xd = X0, then `ace.restrict` performs the `ace.reset` operation.


* `Xs`._AlgorithmPolicy_ is used to set the new `mh`._AlgorithmPolicy_ of the
  CR. No currently disabled operation may be enabled, any attempt to do so raises
  exception `ace_exc_invalid`. In some cases, a zero value is not permitted, such as
  disabling both encryption and decryption.

* `Xs`._SCProtection_ is used to turn on side-channel protection or raise its
  level. Attempting to remove or downgrade side channel protection, or to set a
  non-implemented level raises exception `ace_exc_invalid`.

* If `Xs`._Locality_ is non-zero, then it can be used to restrict the Locality.
  Allowed replacements of Locality are:

 ** _SiPScrt_ {rightarrow} _ChipFamScrt_, _ChipScrt_;
 ** _ChipFamScrt_ {rightarrow} _ChipScrt_;
 ** _OEMScrt_ {rightarrow} _ProdScrt_, _DevScrt_;
 ** _ProdScrt_ {rightarrow} _DevScrt_;
 ** _MLocality_ {rightarrow} _HLocality_, _SLocality_; and
 ** _HLocality_ {rightarrow} _SLocality_.

tweak the export and import algorithms. Generalizing the Locality is not
permitted. Attempting to do so raises exception `ace_exc_invalid`.

* A zero in any of the three bits `Xs`._UsagePolicy_ means that the
  corresponding policy is _not_ changed.

* A one in any of the three bits `Xs`._UsagePolicy_ means that usage by the
  corresponding mode is not allowed, and the corresponding bit in the Metadata
  of the destination CC is set to 1.

* If `Xs`._ExpirationDate_ is zero, the corresponding policy in
  `mh`._ExpirationDate_ is _not_ changed.

* A non-zero value in `Xs`._ExpirationDate_ will be copied to
  `mh`._ExpirationDate_, but only if that policy was not already programmed before
  or the original value of `mh`._ExpirationDate_ was equal or larger.
  Otherwise exception `ace_exc_invalid` is raised.
--
+
On RV32:

* `s` must be even, the register pair `X[s]` and `X[s+1]` are read, and
  `(X[s+1] @ X[s])` replaces `Xs` in the RV64 description.

[NOTE]

This instruction enables several practical use cases:

*   Enabling side-channel protection when supported by the microarchitecture,

*   Allowing privileged code to give a CC to several processes in a

resources by avoiding additional round-trips to trusted environments.

    { bits:  1, name: 'r' },
    { bits:  1, name: 0x0 },

    { bits:  2, name: 0x0, attr: ['func2'] },

* `F` = `0b00`, `r` = `0` {implies} Form A: Size from a context given as `Ks`.
* `F` = `0b00`, `r` = `1` {implies} Form A: Size from a context given indirectly as `K(Xs)`.
* `F` = `0b01`, `r` = `0` {implies} Form B: Size from Metadata Section.

_Description_:: {empty}

* Form A `ace.size` returns the total size (in bytes) of the memory buffer that
  would be necessary to store the exported SCC, including the Additional Data.
  Knowing this size is necessary
  for any feature that relies on `ace.export`, including context switching. The
  instruction returns 0 if the CR is unconfigured.

* Form B `ace.size` returns the total size (in bytes) of an SCC having the
  Metadata section given in `Xn` into GPR `Xd` including the Additional Data,
  `NOT(bin(ace_state_not_impl, XLEN))` if the algorithm is not
  supported, and `NOT(bin(ace_state_invalid, XLEN))` if the Metadata is invalid.
 +
On RV32, `s` must be even and `Xs` and `X[s+1]` contain bits [31:0] and [63:32] of the Metadata.

* Form C: The source register contains the desired byte length of the buffer to
  be allocated, and the destination register contains the size of the buffer in

NOTE: In general, no correlation may be assumed between the size of a PI, the
corresponding CR's usage of internal resources, and the size of the SCC.

[[ACE-interruptibility]]
=== Interruptibility

Except for `ace.import`, `ace.export`, `ace.prov`, `ace.input`, and
`ace.output`, all ACE operations are uninterruptible.

The operation of `ace.exec` on a basic processing unit, in any state of any
algorithm, is uninterruptible, unless explicitly allowed by the algorithm. When
`ace.exec` operates iteratively on any inputs whose length is an integer
multiple of the basic processing unit, each block is processed uninterruptibly,
but the instruction may be interrupted between blocks; the hardware sets
`acestart` to the interruption point and possibly additional information.

The interruptibility of provisioning, import, and export operations is defined
in <<ACE-provisioning-export-import>>.

An implementation must wait before accessing a CR that is still asynchronously
being written to.

[WARNING]
This is takes from a github comment and must be rewritten: +
In `ace.exec`, the primitives themselves are not interrupted, so round number or round key value are not needed.
An implementation can store and cache internally a key expansion, and they could also save it in the optional data section of a Context (but recreating the key expansion would be faster than decrypting anyway...). +
However, if an interrupt occurs WHILE processing, say, an AES operation, the implementation has two choices: either to continue and take the state available only then, or to discard the partially executed operation. acestart in the two cases will either point to the next block or to the discarded block, which is resumed. Only that block will be resumed, not necessarily the entire operation.

[[ACE-provisioning-export-import]]
=== Provisioning, Export and Import of Cryptographic Contexts

The length of a PI must be a multiple of 8 bytes. The first 8 bytes contain the
*Metadata* Header, as defined in <<ACE-Metadata>>. The subsequent data contain
the *Content*, key material and any algorithm-specific data used to initialize
the CR. The Content's format is entirely determined by the _Algorithm_ and
_KeyType_ fields within the Metadata, and is specified in <<ACE-algorithms>>
for each architected algorithm.

Format of Provisioning Input:::
`bytes{nbsp}[{nbsp}0 .. 7{nbsp}{nbsp}{nbsp}]{nbsp}{nbsp}` Metadata Section. +
`bytes{nbsp}[{nbsp}8 .. u-1{nbsp}]{nbsp}{nbsp}` PI Content: Keys and other secrets. Here, `u` = `len_in_bytes(PI)`. +

{empty}
(((Cryptographic Context, Sealed)))
The SCC format is used when exporting a CC from a CR and also to re-import the
CC into a CR. Its size must be a multiple of 8 bytes. The first 8 bytes contain
the Metadata Section, as defined in <<ACE-Metadata>>. The format and length of
the exported Content are algorithm-specific (<<ACE-algorithms>>) and, excluding
the Additional Data of length `mh`._AddDataSize_, are determined solely by
the _Algorithm_, _KeyType_, and _AlgorithmPolicy_ fields in the Metadata.

Format of Sealed Cryptographic Context:::
`bytes{nbsp}[{nbsp}{nbsp}0 .. 7{nbsp}{nbsp}{nbsp}]{nbsp}{nbsp}` Metadata Section, not encrypted. +
`bytes{nbsp}[{nbsp}{nbsp}8 .. 23{nbsp}{nbsp}]{nbsp}{nbsp}` SIV produced by the encryption method (128 bits). +
`bytes{nbsp}[{nbsp}24 .. u-1{nbsp}]{nbsp}{nbsp}` Encrypted Serialized Content of the CR. Here, `u` = `len_in_bytes(SCC)`. +

The entire Metadata and Content are authenticated, but only the Content is
encrypted. The AEAD method used for this purpose is described in
<<ACE-SCC-AEAD>>.

The Serialized Content section under Data Structures in an algorithm
specification (<<ACE-algorithms>>) defines the structure of the `content[]`
array. Architecturally, `content[]` is the serialization of the content of a CR
for export as an SCC.

[NOTE]
--
While `content[]` provides a view of (certain fields of) the content of a CR
that matches the serialized SCC format, it need not be converted to and from
the internal representation and explicitly stored in the ACE unit's memory
before export and after import.

`content[]` is a virtual representation: its byte ordering does not imply
physical contiguity within the CR, nor is there a direct byte-for-byte
correspondence. Changes to `content[]` are reflected in the underlying CR
representation (except for excluded fields), and vice versa.

In a threshold implementation, a single key byte in `content[]` may correspond
to multiple bytes across the key shares within the CR. Reading that byte may
return the XOR of the corresponding bytes in the shares; writing to it may
distribute the value across the corresponding bytes in the shares such that
their XOR equals the input. These operations may be deferred until the end of
an provisioning or import instruction: writing to `context[]` may initially
write only to the first share of a key, and at the end of the provisioning or
import instruction, the other shares would be generated.
--

[[ACE-system-keys]]
==== System Keys and Random System-Generated Key Values

* System Keys are given by an index into an internal, system-specific System
  Keys Table (SKT), and therefore, in a PI or in a SCC, in place of the key
  value itself, a 64-bit field is used to store the index.

* For threshold implementations of system keys, ACE may copy the key material
  from the SKT into the CR and expand it into multiple shares. However, the key
  must be still be exported as a single index.

* It is permissible to include some of the shares of a system key in a
  threshold implementation in the optional Additional Data of the content in
  order to accelerate share reconstruction, provided that the key cannot be
  reconstructed from these shares.

* When the 64-bit index system key index in the PI read nby `ace.prov` is an
  all all-ones value (i.e., `ones(64)`), it denotes that the key material must
  be generated randomly and, similarly to the behaviour of `ace.import.S`, the
  _KeyType_ field of the metadata is internally set to 0, just as for any other
  explicitly provisioned key, and exported as such, i.e., by value. This
  applies to all key material in all algorithms, unless otherwise specified.

[[ACE-size-functions]]
==== Notation for the Size Functions

Let `mh` denote a Metadata Section. In order to determine the total lengths of
the PI and of the SCC, the implementation provides the following functions or
an equivalent mechanism:{empty}

. ``MH_size(mh)`` returns the byte length of the Metadata Section. In the
  current specification, it is always 8.

. ``PI_content_size(mh)`` returns the byte length of the content section of a
  PI. It depends only on `mh`._Algorithm_, `mh`._AlgorithmPolicy_, and
  `mh`._KeyType_.

. ``SCC_content_size(mh)`` returns the byte length of the fixed part of the
  content section of an SCC. It depends only on  `mh`._Algorithm_,
  `mh`._AlgorithmPolicy_, and `mh`._KeyType_. It does not include the length of
  the Additional Data of the Content, of length _AddDataSize_ * 8 bytes.

The total size in bytes of a PI is therefore

`{fournbsp}{fournbsp}len_in_bytes(PI){nbsp}` = `MH_size(mh)` + `PI_content_size(mh)`,

and the total size in bytes of an SCC is

`{fournbsp}{fournbsp}len_in_bytes(SCC)` = `MH_size(mh)` + 16 + `SCC_content_size(mh)` + _AddDataSize_ * 8.

[[ACE-interruption-points]]
==== Interruption Points

Provisioning, export, and import are interruptible only before any memory
access they perform. This means that interrupts other than memory exceptions
must wait until the next memory access.

Provisioning and import instructions may resume from the point of interruption
only if control returns directly after the interrupt handler completes. The
privileged interrupt handler must guarantee restart only in this case. In any
other circumstance, including a context switch, the instruction must be
restarted, and if it is `ace.prov` or `ace.import`, the target CR must also be
cleared before restarting.

An export may resume only if the CR has not been modified before returning to
the original control flow.

The implementation will restart any of these operations if the CR is not in a
valid interrupted state.

The initial SIV computation of `ace.export` may be implemented as either
uninterruptible or interruptible. If uninterruptible, interrupt handlers may
need to wait for completion before accessing the CR, potentially creating
performance bottlenecks or timing issues. To mitigate these issues, this
computation can be made interruptible. as described following the `POLYVAL`
function specification in <<ACE-SCC-POLYVAL>>.

function ace.prov(&CR, M[]) = {

    raise exception ace_exc_invalid;

  - Complete Metadata parsing.
  // NEXT OPERATION PREEMPTIBLE WITH acestart[XLEN-9:0] IN [8 .. Z+7]
  - Sequentially read and parse the z bytes at M+8.

  acestart[XLEN-1:XLEN-8] ← zeros(8);

[[ACE-SCC-AEAD]]
==== Key Derivation and POLYVAL

AES-GCM-SIV cite:[DBLP-journals-iacr-GueronLL17,RFC8452] is used to export and
import CRs.

[WARNING]
AES-GCM-SIV has been chosen because it only required single passes of data in
main memory, reducing the number of external memory accesses. GCM uses also a
single pass but requires separate nonce and tag values, increasing the size of
the exported data by one block.

We define the Key Derivation (<<ACE-SCC-RFC8452-derivation>>) and POLYVAL
(<<ACE-SCC-POLYVAL>>) functions first. The generation of an SCC from a CR
(<<ACE-SCC-export>>) and the import of an SCC into a CR (<<ACE-SCC-import>>)
are defined next.

[[ACE-SCC-RFC8452-derivation]]
.RFC8452 Key Derivation
[]
----
function RFC8452_KeyDeriv(key : bits(256)) = {
  struct {
    AESE256(key, (zeros(125) @ 0b101))[63:0] @ AESE256(key, (zeros(125) @ 0b100))[63:0] @
    AESE256(key, (zeros(125) @ 0b011))[63:0] @ AESE256(key, (zeros(125) @ 0b010))[63:0],
    AESE256(key, (zeros(125) @ 0b001))[63:0] @ AESE256(key, (zeros(125) @ 0b000))[63:0]
  }
}
----

// NOTE: Due to the computational cost of the derivation functions,
implementations may cache recently used derived keys.

`POLYVAL` is a so-called Almost XOR-Universal (AXU) Hash Function. The SIV is
the encryption of the computation of this AXU hash on the concatenation of the
Locality Secrets, the Metadata zero-padded to 128 bits, and the Content of the
CR.

In the pseudocode below for POLYVAL, the data to be digested by POLYVAL is
represented an array with an integral number of 16-byte blocks, where the last
one may be just 8 bytes long.

[[ACE-SCC-POLYVAL]]
.Polyval Hashing Function
[]
----
function POLYVAL(&tmp : bits(128), auth_key : bits(128), blocks[0 .. n-1] : bits(128),
                 last_block_fractional : bool) = {
  tmp ← zeros(128);
  local i : int;
  foreach(i from 0 to n-1) {
    if ((i == n-1) and last_block_fractional) {
      tmp[63:0] ← tmp[63:0] xor blocks[i];
      tmp ← Montmul(tmp, auth_key);
    } else {
      tmp ← tmp xor blocks[i];
      tmp ← Montmul(tmp, auth_key);
    }
  }
}
----

[[ACE-SCC-export]]
==== Export

During SCC export and import, the Locality Secrets associated with the
Localities encoded in the _Locality_ field of the Metadata are prepended, in
ascending index order, to the input of the AEAD tag computation, before the
metadata. If the _Locality_ field is zero, no Locality Secrets are prepended.

The export operation maintains a per-CR flag indicating whether an export is in
progress. This flag prevents the resumption of an export after an instruction
that modifies the CR's internal state is performed. If this scenario were
allowed, resuming the export could compute an incorrect SIV for the remaining
SCC data, causing subsequent import authentication failures. Consequently, any
operation that modifies the CR's internal state clears this flag. If an attempt
is made to resume an export operation when the flag is cleared, the export
restarts from the beginning.

// These variables are kept by the microarchitecture internally, and we need
// one instance per hart, we assume only one export/import is performed.
// per hart at any given time, which means that there cannot be two interrupted
// export or import operations.

[[ACE-SCC-export-listing]]
.Generation of an SCC from a CR
[]
----
function ace.export(&CR, M[]) = {
  mh : bits(64) = & CR.Metadata;           // By reference, from the CR
  external CSK : bits(256);
  external enc_key : bits(256);            // RFC8452_KeyDeriv(CSK)
  external auth_key : bits(128);

  acestart[XLEN-1:XLEN-1] ← 1;
  acestart[XLEN-2:XLEN-8] ← bin(CR,7);

  local SIV, tmp : bits(128);
  local z : int ← SCC_content_size(mh);
  z ← z + mh._AddDataSize_ * 8;

  // CR.content[] includes the optional Additional Data at the end
  CR.content = array of z bytes;
  // In external memory: Metadata Section, then SIV, then Content
  M = array of MH_size(mh) + 16 + z bytes.

  local let last_block_fractional : Boolean ← z % 16 != 0;
  local let num_blocks : int ← ceil(z / 16);
  local L : bits(MAX_LOCALITIES*128);      // Currently, MAX_LOCALITIES = 3
  local num_localities : int ← 0;

  foreach(i from 0 to number of localities - 1) {
    if (CR.Metadata.Locality includes locality[i]) {
      L[num_localities * 128 + 127:num_localities * 128] ← LST[i];
      num_localities ← num_localities + 1;
    }
  }

  POLYVAL(&tmp, auth_key, L[num_localities*128-1:0] @ zeros(64) @ mh @ CR.content[],
           last_block_fractional);
  SIV ← AESE(enc_key, 0 @ tmp[126:0]);

  M[0 .. 7]  ← mh;  // PREEMPTIBLE MEMORY WRITE, WITH acestart[XLEN-9:0] FROM 0 TO 7
  M[8 .. 23] ← SIV; // PREEMPTIBLE MEMORY WRITE, WITH acestart[XLEN-9:0] FROM 8 TO 23

  foreach(i from 0 to num_blocks - 1) {
    if ((i == num_blocks - 1) and last_block_fractional)
      // PREEMPTIBLE MEMORY WRITE, WITH acestart[XLEN-9:0] FROM 24+i*16 TO 31+i*16
      M[24+i*16 .. 31+i*16] ← CR.content[i*16 .. 7+i*16]
               xor AESE(enc_key, 1 @ SIV[126:32] @ (SIV[31:0]+bin(i,32) % 2**32)[63:0];
    else
      // PREEMPTIBLE MEMORY WRITE, WITH acestart[XLEN-9:0] FROM 24+i*16 TO 39+i*16
      M[24+i*16 .. 39+i*16] ← CR.content[i*16 .. 15+i*16]
               xor AESE(enc_key, 1 @ SIV[126:32] @ (SIV[31:0]+bin(i,32) % 2**32);
  }

  acestart[XLEN-1:XLEN-8] ← zeros(8);
}
----

Optional Feature: Interruption of ace.export:::

Interrupting `ace.export` after the key derivation may be possible only
immediately before the first block to the input of `POLYVAL` is processed,
resulting in a `acestart` value of 1. `POLYVAL` itself cannot be interrupted
between the two arithmetic operations inside the loop. If `POLYVAL` is
interrupted after the `montmul` operation, the value of `acestart` will be
16{interpunct}(`i`+1)+1, the number of bytes absorbed so far, plus 1.

[[ACE-SCC-import]]
==== Import

The import algorithm needs some special care:

* The first block read from memory by the operation is the Metadata Section
  `mh`:
 ** If strictly fewer than four bytes are read before the instruction is
    interrupted, ACE leaves `acestart` at 0, and the instruction will be
    restarted upon resumption.
 ** If four bytes or more have been read, the value of `mh.State` in the
    SCC is known. In this case, the instruction may be restarted WITH
    acestart[XLEN-9:0] {ge} 4 recording the amount of data read.
* At the beginning of the import operation, the hardware saves the
  `State` value from the original metadata and sets `mh.State <-
  ace_state_invalid`. At the end of the import operation, `mh.State` is
  set to the saved value.
** Since context switching code cannot export a partially imported CR when
   saving the state, it will clear the CR instead, forcing the hardware to
   restart the import operation.
** Exceptions that immediately return to the interrupted operation, such as
   memory exceptions, may cause the hardware to resume the operation instead,
   if the CR was touched.

[NOTE]
====
Emulating parts of the `ace.export` instructions requires
implementation-specific operations from `ace.exec`. Specifically, an
implementation will have to define ACE operations to perform the decryption of
the block, and write the result to a vector or general-purpose register. The
contents of the vector or general-purpose register can then be stored using a
standard store operation. Such operations can not be normative as they depend
on how the ACE unit is implemented.
====

// *** The hardware will only resume the import operation if the flag is set,
// otherwise it will restart the operation.
// CR.importing is always false except during import.
// During import, the original State is saved and temporarily replaced in the CR
// with ace_state_invalid to prevent any USAGE operations.  Any operation that writes
// to the CR will set CR.importing to false: this allows context switching code to reuse
// the CR and, upon returning to the interrupted import, the hardware will detect
// CR.importing == false and restart the import instead of resume it.  OTOH, if the
// interrupt was caused by a memory exception, the instruction can be resumed.

[[ACE-SCC-import-listing]]
.Importing an SCC into a CR
[]
----
function ace.import(&CR, M[]) = {
  // M : array of bytes;   from memory
  local saved_SN : bits(4);                // This is a "hidden" state as well
  local SIV, tmp : bits(128);

  external CSK : bits(256);
  external enc_key : bits(256);
  external auth_key : bits(128);

  acestart[XLEN-1:XLEN-1] ← 1;
  acestart[XLEN-2:XLEN-8] ← bin(CR,7);

  // PREEMPTIBLE MEMORY READ, WITH acestart[XLEN-9:0] FROM 0 TO 7
  tmp[63:0] ← M[0 .. 7];         // read Metadata Section

  saved_SN ← tmp[63:0].State;
  CR.Metadata ← tmp[63:0];
  CR.Metadata.State ← ace_state_interrupted;

  & mh = CR.Metadata;

  z ← SCC_content_size(mh);
  z ← z + mh._AddDataSize_ * 8;

  if (z == 0)
    raise exception ace_exc_not_impl;

  if (there is not enough memory for the algorithm encoded in the Metadata)
    raise exception ace_exc_out_of_mem;

  num_blocks ← ceil(z / 16);
  last_block_fractional : Boolean ← (z % 16 != 0);

  // Absorb Locality "prefix" (zero or more 128-bit blocks)
  local L : bits(MAX_LOCALITIES*128); // Currently, MAX_LOCALITIES = 3
  local num_localities : int ← 0;
  tmp ← zeros(128);
  foreach(i from 0 to number of localities - 1) {
    if (CR.Metadata.Locality includes locality[i]) {
      tmp ← tmp xor LST[i];
      tmp ← Montmul(tmp, auth_key);
    }
  }

  // PREEMPTIBLE MEMORY READ, WITH acestart[XLEN-9:0] FROM 8 TO 23
  SIV ← M[8 .. 23];
  tmp ← tmp xor (zeros(64) @ mh);
  tmp ← Montmul(tmp, auth_key);

  foreach(i from 0 to num_blocks - 1) {
    if ((i == num_blocks - 1) and last_block_fractional) {
      // PREEMPTIBLE MEMORY READ, WITH acestart[XLEN-9:0] FROM 24+i*16 TO 31+i*16
      content[i*16 .. 7+i*16] ← M[24+i*16 .. 31+i*16]
          xor AESE(enc_key, 1 @ SIV[126:32] @ (SIV[31:0]+bin(i,32) % 2**32)[63:0];
      tmp[63:0] ← tmp[63:0] xor content[i*16 .. 7+i*16];
    } else {
      // PREEMPTIBLE MEMORY READ, WITH acestart[XLEN-9:0] FROM 24+i*16 TO 39+i*16
      content[i*16 .. 15+i*16] ← M[24+i*16 .. 39+i*16]
          xor AESE(enc_key, 1 @ SIV[126:32] @ (SIV[31:0]+bin(i,32) % 2**32);
      tmp ← tmp xor content[i*16 .. 15+i*16];
    }
    tmp ← Montmul(tmp, auth_key);
  }
  tmp ← AESE(enc_key, 0 @ tmp[126:0]);

  if (tmp != SIV) {
    mh ← zeros(64)
    mh.State ← ace_state_import_auth
    Clear all Contents and possibly release their memory
    acestart[XLEN-1:XLEN-8] ← zeros(8);
    return
  }

  - Now the content can be deserialized.
  - This includes computing any derived values not saved as part of the SCC,
  - as well as creating shares for threshold implementations.

  // FINALLY, restore the State from the SCC
  mh.State ← saved_SN;

  acestart[XLEN-1:XLEN-8] ← zeros(8);
}
----

[NOTE]
====
The top 8 bits of `acestart` indicate whether a provisioning, import, or export
operation is in progress, eliminating the need for separate `provisioning`,
`importing`, or `exporting` bits per CR. This design also means that only one
such operation can be interrupted at a time.

An architecture could theoretically support tracking multiple interrupted
import/export operations simultaneously. This would require storing copies of
the `SIV` and `tmp` values (256 bits) along with other small quantities for
each interrupted operation. Since interrupted import/export operations are
uncommon and would only be significant for very large states—of which few would
be in flight simultaneously—this capability is perceived as not necessary. It
is the responsibility of the exception handlers to fully erase `acestart` in
these cases to prevent unpredictable behaviour because of the restart of
interrupted privisioning or import, or of the export of a CR which has been
modified in the meantime.
====

[NOTE]
====
Similar to `ace.export`, `ace.import` requires implementation-specific
`ace.exec` operations to decrypt data from a register into the CR.
====

[[ACE-invalidation]]
=== CRs in Completed, Failed, or Error States

[WARNING]
====
This section is being replaced with a broader treatment of _error states_.
====

{empty}
(((ACE, algorithm, invalid state)))
(((Context Register, invalidation)))
In certain situations, an ACE instruction may cause a CR to enter an error state.
Such states are characterized by values of the State field of the Metadata Section as in <<ACE-states-error>>.
This may occur also as a consequence of an explicit `ace.setst` instruction.

This means that the following happens:

* The content of the CR may be in an undefined state, and can no longer be used.
  The implementation may release the internal resources used by the Content of the
  CR, including any Additional Data, without the need to use `ace.clear` first.
  In the latter case, the _AddDataSize_ field of the Metadata Section is zeroed.

* In particular, if the new State is `ace_state_invalid`, we say that the CR is _invalidated_.

* For instructions that refer to that CR:
 ** `ace.prov`, `ace.reset`, `ace.import`, `ace.export`, `ace.getmd`, `ace.size` and `ace.avail` work normally.
 ** `ace.setst` can only be used as `ace.clear/ace.reset` in which case the CR returns to state _Off_.
 ** All other operations, such as`ace.exec`, `ace.clone`, and `ace.restrict` do not perform any operation in the ACE unit and
 in particular do not change the State field of the CR's metadata.

In States _Success_ and _Failed_, only `ace.prov`, `ace.export`, `ace.import`, `ace.clear`, and `ace.getst` are permitted.
